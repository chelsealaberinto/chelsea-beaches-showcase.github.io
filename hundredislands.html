Hundred Islands National Park

Pangasinan, Philippines

About

Hundred Islands National Park is one of the Philippines’ most remarkable natural treasures, located in Alaminos City, Pangasinan. Comprising 124 islands and islets at low tide (and 123 at high tide), this protected area spans over 1,800 hectares of land and sea. Renowned for its crystal-clear waters, powdery white sand beaches, and vibrant marine life, the park is a favorite destination for both local and international tourists seeking adventure, relaxation, and eco-tourism experiences. It is considered the first national park in the Philippines, established in 1940, highlighting its historical and ecological significance. Each island has its own unique charm — from limestone cliffs and caves to hidden lagoons and snorkeling spots teeming with coral reefs and tropical fish.

Location

Hundred Islands National Park is situated off the coast of Alaminos City, along the Lingayen Gulf in western Pangasinan. The islands are scattered across tranquil blue-green waters, forming a breathtaking seascape that stretches as far as the eye can see. The most famous islands — Governor’s Island, Quezon Island, Marcos Island, and Children’s Island — are developed for tourism, offering facilities like viewing decks, picnic areas, and rest cottages. Other smaller islands remain untouched, preserving the park’s raw natural beauty and biodiversity. The surrounding waters are rich fishing grounds, and the nearby coastal villages offer visitors a glimpse of local life and Ilocano hospitality.

Nature and Environment

Hundred Islands National Park is a biodiversity hotspot. The islands are made of ancient coral limestone shaped by centuries of erosion and rising sea levels. Underwater, the park teems with marine life — including giant clams, sea cucumbers, angelfish, parrotfish, and sea turtles. The park’s ecosystem also supports various bird species and native flora, including mangroves that protect the coastline and serve as nurseries for aquatic life. Environmental efforts led by the City Government of Alaminos and DENR (Department of Environment and Natural Resources) ensure sustainable tourism through strict waste management, coral reef protection, and community participation programs.

Ambiance and Experience

The ambiance of Hundred Islands is a perfect mix of serenity and adventure. Tourists can hop from island to island, discovering hidden caves, diving spots, and white sand coves. Snorkeling and scuba diving reveal colorful coral gardens and schools of tropical fish, while kayaking and banana boat rides provide fun and excitement on the surface. Visitors can climb to the Governor’s Island View Deck for a panoramic view of the islets scattered across the gulf — especially stunning during sunrise and sunset. The nights are peaceful, with the sound of gentle waves and the glow of starlit skies creating a relaxing tropical retreat.

Accommodations and Tourism

Visitors can stay in island cottages, floating huts, or beachfront resorts within Alaminos City. Quezon Island and Governor’s Island offer facilities for overnight camping and picnics, while local resorts on the mainland provide modern comforts and easy access to boat rentals and tour services. Tourism activities are well-organized by the Hundred Islands Protected Area Management Board, which ensures the safety and preservation of the site. Fresh seafood, locally made souvenirs, and friendly tour guides make the experience more memorable. Hundred Islands National Park continues to be a model of eco-tourism — balancing conservation with recreation, allowing visitors to explore its timeless natural beauty while preserving it for future generations.
